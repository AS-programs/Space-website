What is a star??

A star is a huge ball of gas and plasma which produces its own light.Its estimated that there are about 10^24 stars in the observale universe.The
Stars are formed through nebulas.Nebulas are very large(some are even few hundred light years across) bodies of gases.Due to gravity the gases in a
nebula begin to clump together.This keeps going on until the core of the star gets hot and dense enough to start nuclear fusion.Nuclear fusion in the
core of stars starts at about 15 million kelvin.In the core of stars hydrogen atoms are ionized into protons(the electron escapes).These protons bump
into each other and sometimes combine to form a deuterium hydrogen isotope.Another proton combines with this deuterium to form a helium3 atom.Two
helium3 atoms combine to form a 4helium atom and 2 protons.In this reaction the mass of the products is slighly less than the reactants.So where did this
mass go?According to Albert Einstein's formula E=mc^2 mass can be converted into a lot of energy.So this lost mass has been converted into energy(heat
and light).This is what causes the stars to glow.

Classification of stars-

In astronomy classification of stars is done based on observations of their light which can give us information about their temperature and composition.
Previously stars were classified on the basis of their colors.However today they are classified on the basis of their surface temperaturs.The Morganâ€“Keenan
system uses the letters O,B,A,F,G,K and M from the hottest(O type) to the coolest(M type).Each letter class is then subdivided using a numeric digit with 0
being hottest and 9 being coolest (e.g., A8, A9, F0, and F1 form a sequence from hotter to cooler).Also in MK system a luminosity class is added based on
the width of certain absorbtion line in the spectrum.Luminosity classes 0 and I apply to hyper- or supergiants; classes II, III and IV apply to bright, regular
giants, and subgiants, respectively; class V is for main-sequence stars; and class VI and VII apply to subdwarfs and dwarf stars.


But how do we know the temperature of a star??

It is not possible to physically measure the temperature of a star.So we have to resort to observing its light spectrum.Wien's law states that the
wavelengths of light emitted by a blackbody will depend on the temperature of the object.Astronomers measure the wavelengths coming from a star find
its peak and based on the data,they determine its type.

Also hotter stars usually have more mass and volume.This mass increases the pressure and hence the heat.As the stars get hotter they
emit more blue light.Hot stars also have a shorter lifespan as they burn(nuclear fusion not combustion) through their fuel(hydrogen) more

All of this data regarding temperature and luminousity can be represented on a Hertzsprung Russell Diagram(H-R diagram).In this diagram
the horizontal axis shows temperature decreasing to the right and vertical axis shows luminosity increasing upwards.The curve in the middle
shows main sequence stars which account for 90% of the stars in the universe.The sun is a G-type main sequence star.
